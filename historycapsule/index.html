The History of Computers | 60-70s

Artifacts

Floppy Disks

A floppy disk, diskette, or floppy diskette is a type of disk storage made from a thin, flexible disk coated with a magnetic storage medium. It is enclosed in a square or nearly square plastic shell lined with fabric to help remove dust from the spinning disk.

Apple I

The Apple I is an early personal computer designed and built by Steve Wozniak and sold by Steve Jobs in the late 1970s. It was the first product of Apple Computer, Inc.

Video Home System

The Video Home System (VHS) is a standard for analog video recording and playback that was introduced by JVC in 1976. It became the dominant home video format for several decades.

Information

The story of computers in the 1960s and 1970s is a story of transformation—moving from giant, room-sized machines owned only by governments and massive corporations to small, personal devices that could fit on a desk. This era laid the foundation for the modern digital world, transitioning computing from "big iron" mainframes to personal computers (PCs).

The 1960s: The Era of Mainframes and Minicomputers

In the early 1960s, computers were rare, expensive, and intimidating machines used primarily for complex calculations, batch data processing, and large business operations like banking. These were "mainframes," epitomized by the dominant IBM System/360, which was released in 1964 and revolutionized industry by allowing businesses to share one powerful computer with hundreds of users via time-sharing. During this time, computer interaction was indirect. Programmers would prepare data on punched cards, hand them to operators, and wait hours or days for the output printout. However, a significant shift began in the mid-1960s with the introduction of "minicomputers" by companies like Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC). While still the size of refrigerators, these machines were smaller, cheaper, and more interactive than mainframes, allowing smaller laboratories and engineering firms to have their own computers.

Early 1970s: The Microprocessor Revolution

The most significant breakthrough occurred in 1971 when Intel released the 4004, the world's first single-chip microprocessor. This "computer-on-a-chip" combined the central processing unit (CPU) onto a tiny piece of silicon. This innovation meant that the expensive, complex circuits of previous computers could now be mass-produced at a low cost. Initially, these microprocessors were used for specialized applications like calculators and traffic light controllers. However, tech enthusiasts quickly realized they could be used to build general-purpose computers.

Late 1970s: The Birth of the Personal Computer

By 1975, the first personal computer, the MITS Altair 8800, was released in kit form for hobbyists to solder together. While it had no keyboard or screen (users programmed it with switches), it sparked massive interest, leading to the creation of hobbyist clubs like the Homebrew Computer Club. The year 1977 marked the true beginning of the home computer revolution with the release of the "1977 Trinity": the Apple II, the Commodore PET 2001, and the Tandy TRS-80. These machines were pre-assembled, affordable, and user-friendly, aimed at individuals rather than large corporations.

  • Apple II: Featured color graphics and a built-in BASIC programming language.
  • TRS-80: Sold through Radio Shack, it was one of the first widely accessible home computers.
  • Commodore PET: A popular all-in-one unit with a built-in monitor.

By the end of the 1970s, the "computer" had moved out of a specialized, locked room and into a box on a desk, setting the stage for the explosive growth of computing in the 1980s.
